Pottery Museum

Organizing a ceramics event and auction

Project Type

Ceramics, Event Hosting

Date

Nov 1, 2025

I began wheel-throwing as a hobby in September 2024. To celebrate my growth over three semesters of classes, I hosted a mini gallery and auction to share 19 custom pieces with my friends. While those specific works have found new homes, the gallery below features my broader collection—capturing everything from my very first attempts at the wheel to my most recent work.

Ceramics

Selected works

This collection follows my journey with clay, from learning the basics to playing around with new techniques. What started as an exploration of simple forms—like cups and trinket dishes—has grown into a love for making larger, more challenging pieces. As I’ve gotten more comfortable at the wheel, I’ve moved into making plant pots, jug vases, and bubble matcha bowls. My most recent favorites are the ones where I mix wheel-thrown shapes with hand-built details to add a touch of whimsy, like my "House on a Hill" bowl and ducky toothbrush holder.

The Vision

Celebrating the good, the bad, and the wobbly

The idea for a gallery night started with a practical problem: my apartment was overflowing with clay. My friends had always been my biggest cheerleaders, constantly asking when I’d start selling my work, but the thought of a formal exhibition felt too stiff. My goal was to showcase the full trajectory of my pottery journey in a fun, casual way. By incorporating some of the first pieces I ever threw, I wanted to celebrate the imperfections that come with learning a new craft—the wobbly cups, experiments gone wrong, and "failed" glazes that were all part of the process.

The Plan

A Night at the "Me-seum"

Since I was bringing together different friend groups, I wanted the event to feel like a mix between a high-end gallery and a casual happy hour. The plan was simple: a "Happy Hour" for guests to mingle and wander through the gallery, followed by an interactive "People’s Choice" poll to crown the crowd favorite. Once the votes were in, we moved to the main event—a live auction. To make it feel official, I served as the auctioneer, sharing the backstory of every piece before "closing the deal" with a DIY gavel made from a lint remover and brown wrapping paper.

To lean into the museum theme, every piece received a hand-written label detailing its clay body, glaze, and dimensions. While the naming process was mostly driven by the "vibe" of each piece, I especially loved giving the sets their own little backstories. A few of my favorites included:

  • Cosmo and Wanda: a galaxy-themed sauce bowl set
  • Rudy and Judy: two stacking cups that looked like a slightly mismatched old married couple
  • Rochelle and Brochelle: a tea set featuring one cup with a broken foot (hence, Bro-chelle).
The Artist Series

Finding new homes

The night culminated in a high-energy auction where every piece found a new home, from the smallest trinket dish to the most mismatched set. To commemorate the night, we started an "Artist Series" by taking a photo of each friend with their newly won pieces. By the end of the evening, all 19 pieces were out in the world and my apartment was finally, miraculously, empty.
